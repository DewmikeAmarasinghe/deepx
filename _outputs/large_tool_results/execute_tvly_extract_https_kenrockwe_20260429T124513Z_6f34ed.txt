I can't vouch for [ads](/about.htm#ads) below. |   This ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 is arguably the most important lens in photography.  Not only is this lens important in photography; its images are the most important in our history. The photographs brought back from our trips to the moon are the best documentation we have of Man's Greatest Achievement. No one has been outside of Earth's orbit, much less to the moon, for over 50 years. [These are the most recent and important photos we have](/apollo/apollo-11/1969-07-20.htm).  This lens is from an era when [full-time career professional photographers](/tech/what-is-a-pro.htm) were common. Pros bought and used the same lenses for decades and decades. This same optical design remained as state-of-the art for over 55 years! Pros would send out lenses for service as needed, but these lenses never needed to be replaced with \"improved\" versions. Rich hobbyists bought these too, often falling for marketing departments' cosmetic changes advertised as \"upgrades,\" but with the many cosmetic variations from 1957 through 2013, *its optics have never changed*. What did change over the years is cosmetics, shutters and filter sizes — but never the optics.  This lens combines flawless optics in a superior all-metal and ergonomically perfect package. The only gotcha is that while this original C version offers a built-in depth-of-field computer and [self-timer](#selftimer) not found in the [newer CF](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) and [CFE versions](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1), these C lenses are all so old that they are much more likely to need an overhaul to bring them back to perfect operation.  This ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 was the main lens for a quarter of a century in the [leading professional camera system](/hasselblad/) most used by artists like [Ansel Adams](https://amzn.to/4aMaVVp), used by NASA for our moon missions and it was used in all of commercial photography for decades. Your favorite classic album cover was probably shot with this lens. I can't possibly describe just how important and excellent is this lens, which is why this review went unwritten for about nine years after I bought mine.  Pros often bought beat-up used [*HASSELBLAD*](/hasselblad/) items and sent them to [*HASSELBLAD*](/hasselblad/) for their fixed-price overhaul service. *HASSELBLAD's* skilled service always brought items back to like-new with all new parts as needed, for a fixed price. Pros bought gear in the worst, cheapest condition possible and had them rebuilt like new by *HASSELBLAD*. Those days are gone, so don't get any ideas.  **T✻** multicoating was added in the 1970s. It was added just before black models became available, so while you can find the occasional silver version with **T✻** multicoating, I've never seen a black one without **T✻** multicoating. As a simple seven-element, five group optical formula, it delivers great images even single-coated, as all are. **T✻** multicoating doesn't add any significant contrast or color boost; it's mostly marketing as far as this simple lens is concerned.  More important than multicoating, the lack of the **T✻** multicoating mark means you have one of the oldest lenses, and it's more likely to need an overhaul.  The first C version, shown here, came in chrome, then black in later years. It's not marked \"C\" anyplace; you just have to know. It takes [B50 bayonet filters](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=B50%20filter&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1).  The [newer CF version](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) changed to a rubber focus ring and takes larger [B60 bayonet filters](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=B60 filter&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1). They have a newer Prontor shutter that also works with the focal-plane shutter *HASSELBLAD*s.  The [newest CFE version](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) added electronic contacts and replaced the metal filter ring with a crappy plastic one — and still uses the same optics!  All these versions use an in-lens mechanical leaf shutter, so flash sync works perfectly at every shutter speed, which is great for [fill flash](/tech/fill-flash.htm) at 1/500.  *HASSELBLAD* stopped making this V system as of 29 April 2013, but that's no big deal because these last forever with [proper service](/tech/repair.htm#gus), and it's easy to get [digital backs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=Hasselblad Digital Back&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) for it. That's right; for the same price as a typical DSLR it's easy to get a used medium format back today; you don't have to hock your [Mercedes](/cars/index.htm#mercedes) to buy a new one anymore.  These 80mm lenses flew numerous Apollo missions and shot on the moon. If you want *HASSELBLAD* Lunar, the lenses that shot almost everything on most missions were this 80mm and the [250mm](/hasselblad/250mm-f56-c.htm); the other lenses rarely flew.  Planning an important, once-in-a-lifetime photo outing and need to be prepared for anything and everything and to make the most important photographs ever produced? All we brought on most flights to the moon were this 80mm, the [250mm](/hasselblad/250mm-f56-c.htm), and that was it.  If you have good eyes and want to see a few of these lenses, just look up at the moon. We left left several of these on the lunar surface, still there today. These original all-metal versions are still referred to as \"C\" today, even though *HASSELBLAD* only used this designation in its literature through about 1967 to differentiate it from the older, shutterless lenses.  Newer versions are suffixed [CF](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) or [CFE](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1). Optics        [specifications](#spex)       [top](#top) [Hasselblad Zeiss Planar 80mm f/2.8 internal construction](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/construction.gif)  **ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 internal construction.**  7 elements in 5 groups.  No aspherical, plastic or ED elements: just great, highly homogenous glass crafted into lenses with extraordinary precision and uniformity.  Dr. Ludwig Bertele's original, simpler PLANAR design dates to 1931 as a 50mm f/2. In 1932 a [50mm f/1.5 design came out, and it's still sold today by ZEISS](/zeiss/zm/50mm-f15.htm). It was a very simple design with few air-to-glass surfaces which made high-speed lenses a reality back before anti-reflection coatings were invented in World War Two.  PLANAR is a trademark of ZEISS. It no longer refers to a particular optical design and instead is used to denote fast normal lenses.  This lens uses a completely different and more complex symmetrical double-GAUSS design from 1957, with more elements and air-glass surfaces than the 1931 PLANARs. Sharpness       [performance](#perf)       [top](#top) [Image sharpness depends more on you than your lens](/tech/unsharp.htm), and [lens sharpness doesn't mean much to good photographers](/tech/lens-sharpness.htm). It's the least skilled hobbyists who waste the most time blaming fuzzy pictures on their lenses, while real shooters know that few photos ever use all the sharpness of which their lenses are capable due to subject motion and the fact that real subjects are rarely perfectly flat.  This *HASSELBLAD* ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 is equally sharp all across the field at every aperture. Corners are as sharp as the center, presuming you're in perfect focus.  It doesn't get sharper as stopped down; go ahead and shoot wide-open if you like. What will improve stopped down is depth of field and evenness of illumination. Yours, like mine, will most likely need an overhaul for accurate and consistent shutter speeds. This is as analog as it gets; every sample will be different and will be different every day and at different temperatures — and every shot will have a slightly different shutter speed due to shot-to-shot variation.  I present these results mostly for references; serious [film shooters](/tech/how-to-shoot-film.htm) all need to know [Zone-system film speed and meter calibration](/tech/film-speed-calibration.htm) as well as [own a shutter tester](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=shutter speed tester&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) for superb results. Otherwise, most people wind up underexposing due to a lack of calibration, which leads to dull gray shadow areas. Most people would be well advised to shoot at half the rated ISO if they haven't run actual [calibrations](/tech/film-speed-calibration.htm).  Not to worry: if you're shooting negative film, you can overexpose by several stops and not even notice a difference in final results so long as you don't underexpose much. Worrying about fractions of a stop only matters if you're shooting transparencies like [Fuji Velvia](/fuji/velvia-50.htm). Imagine the infrared index as 5mm to the right of the regular index; it's where the red depth-of-field indicator goes when set to f/4.  On the [CF](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) and [CFE versions](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) it's marked on their static depth of field scales at f/4, while this lens' active computer lacks that red mark.   Exposure       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top) [Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg)  **ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 EVS & Depth-of-Field Controls.** [bigger](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg).  To set an [EV (exposure value)](/tech/ev.htm) from a [PME](/hasselblad/pme.htm), a [meter knob](/hasselblad/meter-knob.htm) or any other meter that reads in **EV** (or a guess based on experience), press the ridged tab (seen here between **15** and **18**) down towards the camera (it will move the entire aperture ring away from the shutter speed ring to unlock it), and move it until the **▲ (red triangle**) points to the **EV number** you want.  **EV** sets in half-stops: shutter speeds set in full stops and apertures set in half-stops. I find it easier to more the shutter to its clicks and then fine-tune the aperture setting.  The aperture and shutter speed rings are usually locked together. This way you retain the same exposure (**EV**) regardless of how you rotate the ring. Once set, rotating the ring lets you choose different apertures or shutter speeds while retaining the same exposure. Clever, eh?   Depth of Field        [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top) To preview the depth of field, press-in the little lever between SYNCHRO and COMPUR [shown above](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg).  If your lens is cocked, the diaphragm will stop down, and change as you set apertures.  To reset it to stay open, either take the picture, or set the lens to f/2.8, at which point the diaphragm will now stay locked wide-open as you set different apertures.  This C version has an automatic analog computer that calculates the depth of field for a 60 micron circle-of-confusion. This is always displayed by the red bars next to the focus scale. Newer models economized and removed the computer; they have the usual markings. The **V X M** selector sets the flash synchronization and the self timer.  To unlock the **V X M** selector lever, move the little lock lever (left of the **V**) towards the front of the lens.  **X** is normal, for modern studio strobes, [digital backs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=Hasselblad Digital Back&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) and ordinary electronic flash.  **M** is for [M-type flashbulbs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=M%20Flashbulbs&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1). You guys have no idea how tough it was in the old days; you needed different kinds of flashbulbs depending on what kind of shutter you had!  **V** is the self timer. Push the lever all the way towards V, and you'll get an 8 to 10 second mechanical delay after you press the camera's shutter. The self timer always uses X sync when it fires. Remember that you have to move the lock lever towards the front of the lens to unlock this lever.   The Red Bar Above 1, 1/2 and 1/4 Shutter Speeds       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top) This is a reminder to keep holding the shutter button until the exposure completes. If you don't, the blind in the camera body may close before the exposure completes. Don't worry in **Bulb**; the blind stays open until the shutter closes.   Time Exposures       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top) Read **EV** from either a [PME](/hasselblad/pme.htm), a [meter knob](/hasselblad/meter-knob.htm) or any other meter that reads in **EV,** or simply from prior experience.  Set the **EV** on the scale [as shown above](#exposure).  Below **EV 9** the scale will show times longer than one second in green (**B** is two seconds):  [Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg)  **ZEISS PLANAR T✻ 80mm f/2.8 Long Exposure Scale.** [bigger](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg).  The green numbers on the shutter speed scale are the calculated time exposures in full seconds to use with the apertures below them — but since the shutter's longest time is one second, we have to use **Bulb** and time the exposure manually.  In this example I've set **EV 2**, which now shows lots of combinations from **2** seconds at f/2.8, **4** seconds at f/4 all the way to a minute (**60** seconds) at f/16 and two minutes (**125** seconds) at f/22.  Select in your head the combination you want to use. Remember the time and leave the shutter set to **B** as shown above.  Pull the toothed aperture lever on the right towards the camera body to unlock it, then move it to set the aperture you want to use. Once you've moved this, ignore the indications since you've moved it away from the set **EV**.  Now you're at your selected aperture and **Bulb**. To make your exposure with no need for a cable release, of course be sure your camera is cocked and then:  Charge the self timer by pushing the [unmarked lock lever to the left of the **V X M** selector](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg) towards the front of the lens, and then pushing the **V X M** selector gently towards **V**.  On most 500 series cameras, flip down the Time Exposure Catch (the lock lever around the shutter release). This will keep the shutter pressed without having to hold it for your time exposures!  Focus, compose and press the shutter. The self timer buzzes and opens shutter without camera shake some seconds later. Since you flipped down the Time Exposure Catch (the lock lever around the shutter release), the shutter button stays pressed and the shutter stays open.  When your watch, timer or simply counting seconds in your head tells you you're done, put a hat, your hand or a lens cap in front of the lens to stop the exposure, then flip up the shutter lock lever to close the shutter. Don't touch the camera until you've covered it to stop the exposure. Advance your film as always, and you're done: a perfect, vibration-free time exposure using nothing but your *HASSELBLAD* and your skilled, creative hands. I can't vouch for [ads](/about.htm#ads) below.\n\nThis ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 is arguably the most important lens in photography.\n\nNot only is this lens important in photography; its images are the most important in our history. The photographs brought back from our trips to the moon are the best documentation we have of Man's Greatest Achievement. No one has been outside of Earth's orbit, much less to the moon, for over 50 years. [These are the most recent and important photos we have](/apollo/apollo-11/1969-07-20.htm).\n\nThis lens is from an era when [full-time career professional photographers](/tech/what-is-a-pro.htm) were common. Pros bought and used the same lenses for decades and decades. This same optical design remained as state-of-the art for over 55 years! Pros would send out lenses for service as needed, but these lenses never needed to be replaced with \"improved\" versions. Rich hobbyists bought these too, often falling for marketing departments' cosmetic changes advertised as \"upgrades,\" but with the many cosmetic variations from 1957 through 2013, *its optics have never changed*. What did change over the years is cosmetics, shutters and filter sizes — but never the optics.\n\nThis lens combines flawless optics in a superior all-metal and ergonomically perfect package. The only gotcha is that while this original C version offers a built-in depth-of-field computer and [self-timer](#selftimer) not found in the [newer CF](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) and [CFE versions](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1), these C lenses are all so old that they are much more likely to need an overhaul to bring them back to perfect operation.\n\nThis ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 was the main lens for a quarter of a century in the [leading professional camera system](/hasselblad/) most used by artists like [Ansel Adams](https://amzn.to/4aMaVVp), used by NASA for our moon missions and it was used in all of commercial photography for decades. Your favorite classic album cover was probably shot with this lens. I can't possibly describe just how important and excellent is this lens, which is why this review went unwritten for about nine years after I bought mine.\n\nPros often bought beat-up used [*HASSELBLAD*](/hasselblad/) items and sent them to [*HASSELBLAD*](/hasselblad/) for their fixed-price overhaul service. *HASSELBLAD's* skilled service always brought items back to like-new with all new parts as needed, for a fixed price. Pros bought gear in the worst, cheapest condition possible and had them rebuilt like new by *HASSELBLAD*. Those days are gone, so don't get any ideas.\n\n**T✻** multicoating was added in the 1970s. It was added just before black models became available, so while you can find the occasional silver version with **T✻** multicoating, I've never seen a black one without **T✻** multicoating. As a simple seven-element, five group optical formula, it delivers great images even single-coated, as all are. **T✻** multicoating doesn't add any significant contrast or color boost; it's mostly marketing as far as this simple lens is concerned.\n\nMore important than multicoating, the lack of the **T✻** multicoating mark means you have one of the oldest lenses, and it's more likely to need an overhaul.\n\nThe first C version, shown here, came in chrome, then black in later years. It's not marked \"C\" anyplace; you just have to know. It takes [B50 bayonet filters](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=B50%20filter&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1).\n\nThe [newer CF version](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) changed to a rubber focus ring and takes larger [B60 bayonet filters](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=B60 filter&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1). They have a newer Prontor shutter that also works with the focal-plane shutter *HASSELBLAD*s.\n\nThe [newest CFE version](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) added electronic contacts and replaced the metal filter ring with a crappy plastic one — and still uses the same optics!\n\nAll these versions use an in-lens mechanical leaf shutter, so flash sync works perfectly at every shutter speed, which is great for [fill flash](/tech/fill-flash.htm) at 1/500.\n\n*HASSELBLAD* stopped making this V system as of 29 April 2013, but that's no big deal because these last forever with [proper service](/tech/repair.htm#gus), and it's easy to get [digital backs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=Hasselblad Digital Back&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) for it. That's right; for the same price as a typical DSLR it's easy to get a used medium format back today; you don't have to hock your [Mercedes](/cars/index.htm#mercedes) to buy a new one anymore.\n\nThese 80mm lenses flew numerous Apollo missions and shot on the moon. If you want *HASSELBLAD* Lunar, the lenses that shot almost everything on most missions were this 80mm and the [250mm](/hasselblad/250mm-f56-c.htm); the other lenses rarely flew.\n\nPlanning an important, once-in-a-lifetime photo outing and need to be prepared for anything and everything and to make the most important photographs ever produced? All we brought on most flights to the moon were this 80mm, the [250mm](/hasselblad/250mm-f56-c.htm), and that was it.\n\nIf you have good eyes and want to see a few of these lenses, just look up at the moon. We left left several of these on the lunar surface, still there today.\n\n### New since lenses for the old 1000 and 1600 cameras       [intro](#intro)       [top](#top)\n\n![blue ball icon © 1957, with more elements and air-glass surfaces than the 1931 PLANARs.\n\nThe first versions of this 1957 design are single coated mostly in blue, and most later versions are multicoated, which Zeiss brands as **T\\***.\n\nActual focal length: 80.5 mm.\n\nFlange focal distance (common to all lenses for this system): 74.90 mm.\n\n### Close Focus       [specifications](#spex)       [top](#top)\n\n3 it's where the red depth-of-field indicator goes when set to f/4.\n\nOn the [CF](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-cf.htm) and [CFE versions](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=(Hasselblad,Zeiss,Planar) 80mm CFE -(CF,FE,C,HC,Jupiter,FLE,CFi)&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) it's marked on their static depth of field scales at f/4, while this lens' active computer lacks that red mark.\n\n### Exposure       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\n[![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg)](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg)\n\n![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg)\n\n**ZEISS PLANAR 80mm f/2.8 EVS & Depth-of-Field Controls.** [bigger](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg).\n\nTo set an [EV (exposure value)](/tech/ev.htm) from a [PME](/hasselblad/pme.htm), a [meter knob](/hasselblad/meter-knob.htm) or any other meter that reads in **EV** (or a guess based on experience), press the ridged tab (seen here between **15** and **18**) down towards the camera (it will move the entire aperture ring away from the shutter speed ring to unlock it), and move it until the **▲ (red triangle**) points to the **EV number** you want.\n\n**EV** sets in half-stops: shutter speeds set in full stops and apertures set in half-stops. I find it easier to more the shutter to its clicks and then fine-tune the aperture setting.\n\nThe aperture and shutter speed rings are usually locked together. This way you retain the same exposure (**EV**) regardless of how you rotate the ring. Once set, rotating the ring lets you choose different apertures or shutter speeds while retaining the same exposure. Clever, eh?\n\n### Depth of Field       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\nTo preview the depth of field, press-in the little lever between SYNCHRO and COMPUR [shown above](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7562-evs-scale.jpg).\n\nIf your lens is cocked, the diaphragm will stop down, and change as you set apertures.\n\nTo reset it to stay open, either take the picture, or set the lens to f/2.8, at which point the diaphragm will now stay locked wide-open as you set different apertures.\n\nThis C version has an automatic analog computer that calculates the depth of field for a 60 micron circle-of-confusion. This is always displayed by the red bars next to the focus scale. Newer models economized and removed the computer; they have the usual markings.\n\nTo calculate the aperture that will give optimum sharpness when you need depth-of-field and don't want diffraction to soften the image, put a new scale over the computer display and use these half distances (see [here for details of how to use these](/tech/focus.htm)):\n\n**focal\nlength = 80.5 mm** (actual design focal length)\n\n|  |  |  |  |\n| --- | --- | --- | --- |\n| **f/** | **feet** | **meters** | **aperture on Zeiss' computer** |\n| **5.6** | 500 | 152.52 |  |\n| **8** | 250 | 76 | end of aluminum |\n| **11** | 125 | 38.1 | f/2.8 |\n| **16** | 62.6 | 19.1 | f/5.6 |\n| **22** | 31.3 | 9.5 | f/11 |\n\n**f/**\n\n([how to use this](/tech/focus.htm))\n\n### Self Timer, Flash & [Digital Back](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=Hasselblad Digital Back&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) Sync       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\n[![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg)](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg)\n\n![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg)\n\n**ZEISS PLANAR T✻ 80mm f/2.8 Flash Sync & Self-Timer Controls.** [bigger](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg).\n\nThe **V X M** selector sets the flash synchronization and the self timer.\n\nTo unlock the **V X M** selector lever, move the little lock lever (left of the **V**) towards the front of the lens.\n\n**X** is normal, for modern studio strobes, [digital backs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=Hasselblad Digital Back&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1) and ordinary electronic flash.\n\n**M** is for [M-type flashbulbs](https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=M%20Flashbulbs&mkcid=1&mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&siteid=0&campid=5336071501&customid=&toolid=10001&mkevt=1). You guys have no idea how tough it was in the old days; you needed different kinds of flashbulbs depending on what kind of shutter you had!\n\n**V** is the self timer. Push the lever all the way towards V, and you'll get an 8 to 10 second mechanical delay after you press the camera's shutter. The self timer always uses X sync when it fires. Remember that you have to move the lock lever towards the front of the lens to unlock this lever.\n\n### The Red Bar Above 1, 1/2 and 1/4 Shutter Speeds       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\nThis is a reminder to keep holding the shutter button until the exposure completes. If you don't, the blind in the camera body may close before the exposure completes. Don't worry in **Bulb**; the blind stays open until the shutter closes.\n\n### Time Exposures       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\nRead **EV** from either a [PME](/hasselblad/pme.htm), a [meter knob](/hasselblad/meter-knob.htm) or any other meter that reads in **EV,** or simply from prior experience.\n\nSet the **EV** on the scale [as shown above](#exposure).\n\nBelow **EV 9** the scale will show times longer than one second in green (**B** is two seconds):\n\n[![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg)](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg)\n\n![Zeiss 80mm f/2.8 EVS controls](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg)\n\n**ZEISS PLANAR T✻ 80mm f/2.8 Long Exposure Scale.** [bigger](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/OM1D0277-time-exposures.jpg).\n\nThe green numbers on the shutter speed scale are the calculated time exposures in full seconds to use with the apertures below them — but since the shutter's longest time is one second, we have to use **Bulb** and time the exposure manually.\n\nIn this example I've set **EV 2**, which now shows lots of combinations from **2** seconds at f/2.8, **4** seconds at f/4 all the way to a minute (**60** seconds) at f/16 and two minutes (**125** seconds) at f/22.\n\nSelect in your head the combination you want to use. Remember the time and leave the shutter set to **B** as shown above.\n\nPull the toothed aperture lever on the right towards the camera body to unlock it, then move it to set the aperture you want to use. Once you've moved this, ignore the indications since you've moved it away from the set **EV**.\n\nNow you're at your selected aperture and **Bulb**. To make your exposure with no need for a cable release, of course be sure your camera is cocked and then:\n\nCharge the self timer by pushing the [unmarked lock lever to the left of the **V X M** selector](/hasselblad/80mm-f28-c/810_7268-black-self-timer.jpg) towards the front of the lens, and then pushing the **V X M** selector gently towards **V**.\n\nOn most 500 series cameras, flip down the Time Exposure Catch (the lock lever around the shutter release). This will keep the shutter pressed without having to hold it for your time exposures!\n\nFocus, compose and press the shutter. The self timer buzzes and opens shutter without camera shake some seconds later. Since you flipped down the Time Exposure Catch (the lock lever around the shutter release), the shutter button stays pressed and the shutter stays open.\n\nWhen your watch, timer or simply counting seconds in your head tells you you're done, put a hat, your hand or a lens cap in front of the lens to stop the exposure, then flip up the shutter lock lever to close the shutter. Don't touch the camera until you've covered it to stop the exposure. Advance your film as always, and you're done: a perfect, vibration-free time exposure using nothing but your *HASSELBLAD* and your skilled, creative hands.\n\n### Filters       [user's guide](#usage)       [top](#top)\n\nSee [Filters](#filt).\n\n## Recommendations       [top](#top)\n\n[**Sample Images**](#sampleimages) **[Intro](#intro)   [New](#new)   [Good](#good)   [Bad](#bad)   [Missing](#missing)**\n\n**[Compatibility](#compat)   [Format](#format)   [Specifications](#spex)**\n\n**[Performance](#perf)**   [**Compared**](#compared)   **[Usage](#usage)**\n\n**[Recommendations](#rex)   [More](#more)**\n\nThis is the perfect lens for just about everything shot with the [*HASSELBLAD*](/hasselblad/).
